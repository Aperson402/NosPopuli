H.R. 4838 119th Congress · 2025–2026 Passed House

Medicaid Eligibility Streamlining Act

Modernizes how states determine Medicaid eligibility by replacing quarterly income reviews with a single annual review, and requires states to accept federal verification of citizenship status.

Introduced May 3, 2026· 1 sponsor · 41 cosponsors· Referred to Energy & Commerce
Plain English Explanation

What it does. Today, states recheck Medicaid eligibility every three months. This bill makes it once a year, and forces states to trust the federal government's verification that someone is a citizen instead of requiring extra paperwork from the enrollee.

Who it affects. An estimated 14 million Medicaid enrollees who lose coverage temporarily each year due to paperwork churn. State Medicaid offices in 36 states that currently do quarterly reviews. Federal CMS, which would absorb verification costs.

Costs, trade-offs, and obligations. Annual reviews cost states less to administer but the federal share of Medicaid spending rises by an estimated $4.2B over 10 years (CBO). States lose discretion to verify citizenship independently. People who become ineligible mid-year stay enrolled until annual review — a trade-off the sponsor calls "fewer paperwork dropoffs" and critics call "improper payments."

What's next. Now in the Senate, where Finance Committee chair has not yet scheduled a hearing. A companion bill (S. 2104) has 12 cosponsors.
